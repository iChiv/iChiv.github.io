## Overview

**Project Catch** is a collaborative game built around photography, projection, and interaction with the environment. The challenge for me wasn't just coming up with a novel mechanic — it was taking a set of scattered creative keywords and assembling them into a coherent playable system with solid level structure and a working prototype.

Unlike projects that stay at the concept stage, **Project Catch** was a full push from idea to playable form. It required me to simultaneously handle mechanic viability, level expression, programming implementation, and resource coordination within a team.

## Core Mechanics

The core experience follows a logic of: *observe → capture → project → interact with the environment*. I wanted the player to engage with scenes and objects through a lens of "capture and reframe," rather than simply moving through and solving puzzles passively.

The difficulty in designing this mechanic was that it had to be novel enough to feel distinct, but not so abstract that the player wouldn't know how to use it. Too simple, and the project loses its strongest differentiator. Too complex, and the player never gets past the learning curve.

I focused on three things:

- Can the mechanic sustain varied puzzle expressions across the game?
- Do the levels naturally lead the player toward understanding the rules?
- Can the player have "I didn't know I could do that" moments through experimentation?

## My Role

In this project, I was responsible for:

- **Core mechanic and level design**
- **Programming and gameplay implementation**
- Driving **resource integration and prototype delivery** within the team

What made this project meaningful for me is that it tested not just the ability to *propose* gameplay ideas, but the ability to *make them work*. I had to think about how the mechanics and levels hold up from a design perspective, while also turning them into something runnable, testable, and iterable on the implementation side.

## Key Design Decisions

### Making the mechanic support sustained play, not just first impressions

Many mechanics feel interesting for the first five minutes but reveal repetition or thinness once they enter real play. With **Project Catch**, I paid close attention to whether the core loop could keep generating novel puzzle situations, rather than just feeling clever at the start.

### Level design as a teaching instrument

Because the game's rules aren't something players would naturally be familiar with, the levels themselves had to serve pedagogical and validation functions — not just challenge functions. I thought carefully about level ordering, information release, and trial cost so that players would gradually internalize the rules rather than being overwhelmed by complexity up front.

### Driving prototype delivery through collaboration

This wasn't a solo concept demo — it required continuous integration of resources, alignment of direction, and pushing the prototype forward as a team. Beyond mechanics and levels, I invested effort into ensuring that what the team discussed could actually become something playable and worth evaluating, rather than remaining as ideas.

## What I Learned

**Project Catch** deepened my understanding of what it takes to turn a design idea into a working experience. Whether an idea works depends not only on whether it looks novel on paper, but also on whether it can be supported through level design, interaction, and implementation at every layer.

This project also clarified what I'm strongest at:

- Translating abstract concepts into **clear mechanic structures**
- Keeping **design and implementation aligned** through the prototyping phase
- Making gameplay that goes from "runnable" to "understandable and enjoyable for the player" through level and feedback design
