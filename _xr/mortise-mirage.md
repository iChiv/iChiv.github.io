## Overview

**Mortise Mirage** is a VR puzzle game inspired by the structural logic of traditional Chinese mortise and tenon joinery. What mattered most to me in this project was not just making a culturally-themed VR experience — it was addressing a more specific design question: **how do you translate a structural system with cultural semantics into interaction mechanics that a player can intuitively perceive and gradually master in VR?**

## Core Mechanics

The core experience revolves around observing structure, understanding connection relationships, and completing reassembly through hands-on manipulation. Unlike traditional flat puzzle games, VR enables the player to approach, pick up, rotate, and compare components directly — so I focused on making the puzzle logic emerge from the structure itself, rather than from external hints or instructions.

Two design concerns guided the work:

- **Is the structural logic intuitive enough?** Can the player infer the relationship between components through shape, position, and feedback alone?
- **Is the interaction burden reasonable?** VR is immersive by nature, but it also introduces cognitive cost through hand control, perspective, and step complexity. The mechanics had to remain clear enough that the player never lost the thread.

The result is a puzzle rhythm built around structural cognition plus interactive validation: observe, attempt, adjust understanding through feedback — rather than passively receiving a solution.

## My Role

In this project, I was responsible for:

- Connecting **traditional mortise and tenon concepts with VR puzzle mechanics**
- Designing and iterating on **core interaction and gameplay**
- Continuously assessing **immersion, clarity, and pacing** from the player's perspective

My priority was not just generating ideas, but constantly evaluating: which interactions genuinely benefit from VR's affordances, which complexity is worth keeping, and which information needs to reach the player earlier and more clearly.

## Key Design Decisions

### Making cultural content playable rather than decorative

Many culture-themed projects treat the cultural element as visual flavor. I wanted the joinery logic — the connection relationships, the disassembly and reassembly reasoning — to be the puzzle itself, not the background. The player should feel the uniqueness of the theme through interaction, not through exposition.

### Balancing immersion with comprehensibility in VR

VR is well-suited for immersive interaction, but poor information architecture can leave the player disoriented even when they can "see and touch" everything. I paid close attention to the comprehension threshold at each step: does the player know their current objective? Can they read the feedback? Does the interaction itself disrupt the puzzle rhythm?

### Pacing the experience

If the puzzle is too linear, the player loses exploration. If it's too open, VR fatigue sets in quickly. I focused on keeping the player in a state of *having direction but still needing to think* — not pure trial-and-error, and not passive hint-following.

## What I Learned

This project reinforced two things for me:

- **System design**: how to translate abstract rules into interaction logic that the player can master step by step
- **Prototyping iteration**: how to refine an idea from "interesting in concept" to "playable in practice" through repeated adjustments to interaction, feedback, and pacing

It also sharpened my sense of when a mechanic is worth the complexity it introduces — a question I now ask earlier in every project.
